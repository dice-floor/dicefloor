# Casino Advantage by Game: What Every Supervisor Needs to Know

***The math behind why the casino wins — and why understanding it will make you a better manager and your dealers a more effective team***

The moment you step into a supervisory role on the casino floor, your relationship with the games changes. You are no longer just dealing — you are responsible for the results. And to manage results effectively, you need to understand the mathematics behind them. Not at an academic level, and not with a calculator in hand every shift, but well enough to make sound decisions, read what is happening at your tables, and avoid the kind of reactive management that costs both the casino and your team more than a run of bad cards ever will.
This article breaks down two things every casino supervisor should have a firm grip on: the house edge for the main games on your floor, and the standard hands or rounds per hour that determine how much theoretical revenue each table generates. Together, these two figures tell you almost everything you need to know about what is actually happening at a table — and what is not.

**Part One: The House Edge — Why the Casino Always Wins Over Time**

The house edge is the percentage of each wager that the casino expects to retain over time. It is a long-run mathematical certainty, not a guarantee on any individual hand or spin. In the short term, anything can happen — guests win big, tables run cold for an hour, a single player beats the game for a session. None of that contradicts the math. Over thousands of hands and millions of wagers, the edge does its work reliably. Your job as a supervisor is to understand this, trust it, and manage your floor accordingly.

**House Edge by Game**
| **Game** | **House Edge** | **Notes** |
|:-:|:-:|:-:|
| Three Card Poker | 2.0% – 3.4% | Ante & Play bet |
| Five Card Poker | 2.6% – 5.2% | Varies by variant |
| American Roulette | 5.26% | Double zero (0, 00) |
| European Roulette | 2.70% | Single zero |
| Blackjack 4 Deck | ~0.60% | Basic strategy |
| Blackjack 6 Deck | ~0.64% | Basic strategy |
| Blackjack 8 Deck | ~0.65% | Basic strategy |
| Punto Banco / Baccarat | 1.06% – 1.24% | Banker / Player bet |
| Dice (Craps) | 1.36% – 1.41% | Pass / Don't Pass |

*Note: House edge figures assume standard rules and, for Blackjack, basic strategy play. Actual figures vary by specific table rules, payout structures, and casino jurisdiction.*

A few things worth noting from these numbers. Blackjack, across all deck configurations, carries the lowest house edge on this list — but only when players use basic strategy. The more decks in play, the slightly higher the edge, which is why casinos moved from single-deck to multi-deck games over the decades. The difference between four, six, and eight decks is marginal in practice, but it adds up across thousands of hours of play.

European Roulette's 2.70% edge versus American Roulette's 5.26% comes down entirely to the extra double-zero pocket on the American wheel. Same game, same payouts, nearly double the edge. For a busy roulette table, that difference in theoretical win is significant over the course of a week or a month.

Punto Banco and Baccarat sit in a comfortable middle ground — a low edge that makes the game attractive to players, and a fast pace that compensates in volume. Three and Five Card Poker carry higher edges, which is partly why guests tend to play them for shorter sessions. And Craps, often misunderstood as a complicated game with a high edge, is actually one of the better bets on the floor when players stick to Pass Line and Don't Pass wagers — the complexity comes from the proposition bets, which carry edges well above 10%.

**Part Two: Hands Per Hour — Where the Revenue Actually Comes From**

The house edge alone does not tell you how much a table earns. A game with a 5% edge running at 20 hands per hour generates less theoretical revenue than a game with a 1% edge running at 100 hands per hour. The formula that matters is simple: average bet multiplied by hands per hour multiplied by house edge equals theoretical win per player per hour. Speed is the multiplier that turns a mathematical advantage into actual profit.

*Note: All figures are industry averages for guidance only. Actual speeds vary significantly by table occupancy, game intensity, dealer experience, and casino standards. A full Blackjack table will deal fewer hands per hour than a heads-up game; a ceremonial Baccarat table may run well below 60 hands. Use these numbers as a reference point, not a fixed target.*

Blackjack is the clearest example of how table occupancy affects speed. A full seven-player table will typically produce around 50 to 60 hands per hour. The same dealer working heads-up with a single player can produce upwards of 150. Neither situation is inherently better from a management perspective — a full table generates more total action even at a slower pace — but it illustrates why dealer speed and table conditions need to be read together, not in isolation.
Roulette speed depends heavily on how long players take to place their bets and how complex the layout becomes. A straightforward game with a small number of players can spin significantly faster than one where the table is covered in chips and every payout requires careful calculation. European Roulette and American Roulette run at similar speeds — the edge difference between them comes from the wheel, not the pace.

Craps produces a high number of dice rolls per hour, but the complexity of the betting layout means that the time between rolls is often spent managing active bets across multiple players. The pace of the game feels fast, but the revenue per roll depends heavily on average bet size and how many players are active at any given moment.

**What This Means for How You Manage Your Floor**

Here is the practical takeaway that separates effective supervisors from reactive ones: short-term results at a table tell you almost nothing about whether your team is performing well. A Blackjack table that pays out several hands in a row is not a sign that something is wrong. It is variance — the normal statistical noise that exists within any game of chance. The house edge is doing its job, silently, over thousands of hands. Your job is to let it.

The supervisors who panic during losing runs, swap dealers out of fear rather than strategy, or push their team to deal faster than the game naturally allows are not protecting the casino's results — they are undermining them. A dealer who is fast, consistent, and following correct procedures is generating the theoretical win that the math promises. Pulling them mid-shoe because the table has run cold is a management decision based on superstition, not numbers, and it signals to your team that their performance is being judged on factors outside their control.

And your team notices. Dealers who work under managers who understand the math feel supported, because they know their results are being read correctly. Dealers who work under managers who react emotionally to short-term variance feel unfairly judged, and that feeling shows up in their engagement, their energy at the table, and ultimately in the guest experience they provide. Happy, confident dealers attract guests who stay longer and spend more. The correlation is not subtle.

**The Manager Who Knows the Math Wins More**

One of the clearest patterns in casino management is this: the most aggressive, results-obsessed managers — the ones watching every hand, rotating dealers constantly, and treating every cold run as a crisis — consistently underperform the managers who understand the game deeply, trust the edge, and focus their energy on the things that actually move the needle: dealer speed, guest experience, table atmosphere, and staff development.

The math is not working against you. It is working for you, every single shift, at every table on your floor. Your role is to create the conditions in which it can do its job. That means fast, consistent dealers. It means guests who feel welcome enough to stay. It means a team that trusts your judgment because your judgment is based on something real. Know the edge. Know the pace. And manage from that foundation rather than from fear.

The supervisor who is calm, knowledgeable, and consistent is the one every team wants, and the one every smart operator promotes. Be that person. The math will do the rest.
